MAKE AND PROCESS A TOY IMAGE SIGNAL

I made a wedge (200 pix, 20 deg angle span) pointed toward 90 deg, and I characterize that wedge with two types of signals :

  • The spatial pattern formed by the intensities of the pixels that compose the wedge
  • The intensities of the wedge pixels relative to the gray background (the wedge contrast)

Wedge's spatial pattern of intensities. I filled the wedge with random intensity values, and decomposed it in a series of simpler and well formalized sinewave signals with different spatial frequencies. I then removed all the high spatial frequencies to only look at the sinewage components of the wedge with low spatial frequencies (i.e, low-pass filtered the visual signal). The decomposition of the visual signal into sinewave components can be done by the fourier transform (fft2 in matlab)

The wedge is first defined by the 1-valued elements (high intensity) of a 400 rows x 400 columns matrix: >> w = slGraphWedges2(400,400,200,90,20); >> imagesc(w); colormap('gray')

Decompose the visual signal into spatial sinewave components with different frequencies (fourier transformation), create a 2D-Gaussian matrix mask (30 deg std) that overweights zero frequencies (2D Gaussians centered at zero frequencies) and underweights (weights near zero) high frequencies, and apply that mask onto my fourier transformed signa to remove high frequencies and let low frequencies pass through (Hadamard product of the two matrices).

%0 and 1 random intensity matrix >> randIntensities = rand(400,400) > 0.5;

%Keep the low spatial frequency components of the random intensity matrix >> lowFreqComp = slFourierFilt(randIntensities,30,'gausWeights') >> meanwI = mean(mean(lowFreqComp(w==1))); >> wf = repmat(meanwI,400,400); >> w(w==1) = lowFreqComp(w==1);

The wedge contrast (its intensity relative to the background) can also manipulated by simply scaling the max and minimum intensity values of the wedge : e.g., a contrast of 1% is obtained scaling the wedge intensities between $I_{max} = meanwI - 0.01/2$ and $I_{min} = meanwI + 0.01/2$. The contrast are typically quantified and controlled by Weber or Michelson equations $(I - Ib)/Ib)$ and $(Imax - Imin)/(Imax + Imin)$ respectively where $I$ and $I_{max}$ are the max intensities, $I_b$ is the background intensity and $I_{min}$ is the minimal intensity.
